# Gaussian operations on a photonic chip.

<p className="chapter-lede">A continuous-variable photonic processor is built from a surprisingly small alphabet: phase shifters, beam splitters, displacements, and squeezers. Any Gaussian unitary on N modes is a recipe in those four ingredients, courtesy of the Bloch–Messiah and Reck–Clements decompositions. The catch (and there's always a catch) is that a chip restricted to this alphabet, fed Gaussian input, can be simulated on a laptop while you make a coffee.</p>

## Physical intuition: the Mach–Zehnder

A Mach–Zehnder interferometer (MZI) is the smallest programmable Lego brick of a photonic mesh. Light enters two waveguides, a first beam splitter mixes them, a phase shifter slows one arm down a bit, and a second beam splitter recombines them. Twiddling that internal phase sweeps the output between "all the light goes here" and "all the light goes there," continuously and on demand.

So an MZI is really a 2x2 variable beam splitter you can tune with a single DC voltage. Stack a grid of them on a shared waveguide layout, and you get a mesh that can implement any N-mode passive linear-optical unitary you like. Phase shifters set per-path delays; beam splitters mix neighboring paths. That's it. Those are the only knobs in a passive Gaussian circuit.

Squeezers and displacements are the two active extras. A squeezer reshapes the mode's vacuum noise: sharper along one quadrature, blurrier along the perpendicular one. A displacement just shoves the state across phase space by adding a coherent amplitude. Throw squeezers and displacements onto the passive mesh and you can generate the full Gaussian group on N modes.

## Analogy: a programmable optical road network

Picture N parallel roads. Phase shifters are adjustable speed bumps on a single lane, delaying that lane's traffic by whatever amount you like. Beam splitters are tunable T-junctions that redirect a programmable fraction of vehicles from one lane onto the next. Squeezers are weird road segments that asymmetrically stretch and compress vehicle spacing along one axis of position-momentum, like a stretch of highway where everyone tailgates the car in front but leaves enormous gaps to the side.

A circuit spec is a route plan: a sequence of bumps, junctions, and stretch zones that maps an input traffic pattern to an output one. Reck and Clements give two universal layouts for the junction grid, triangular and rectangular respectively, like two different schools of urban planning that both get you to the airport.

**Where the analogy breaks:**

<ul>
  <li>Cars on different lanes do not interfere; optical amplitudes on different modes do, and the splitter mixes complex amplitudes coherently.</li>
  <li>A car takes one route; a photon amplitude takes a superposition over routes, and only the squared modulus shows up in detection statistics.</li>
  <li>Traffic carries no phase; the phase shifter has no road analog beyond a delay, and global phases are not observable.</li>
  <li>Squeezing has no classical traffic analog; it redistributes vacuum fluctuations, which a road network does not have.</li>
</ul>

## Mathematical structure: beam splitters, phase shifters, squeezers

Let <em>a<sub>i</sub></em> and <em>a<sub>i</sub><sup>†</sup></em> denote the annihilation and creation operators of mode <em>i</em>, satisfying [<em>a<sub>i</sub></em>, <em>a<sub>j</sub><sup>†</sup></em>] = δ<sub>ij</sub>. A phase shifter on mode <em>i</em> is U<sub>φ</sub> = exp(iφ <em>a<sub>i</sub><sup>†</sup>a<sub>i</sub></em>), which rotates the mode operator as <em>a<sub>i</sub></em> → e<sup>-iφ</sup><em>a<sub>i</sub></em>.

A beam splitter with transmissivity T = cos²(θ/2) acting on modes (<em>a<sub>1</sub></em>, <em>a<sub>2</sub></em>) is the 2x2 unitary U<sub>BS</sub>(θ, ϕ) = exp(iθ(e<sup>iϕ</sup><em>a<sub>1</sub><sup>†</sup>a<sub>2</sub></em> + e<sup>-iϕ</sup><em>a<sub>1</sub>a<sub>2</sub><sup>†</sup></em>)/2). A displacement on mode <em>i</em> is D(α) = exp(α<em>a<sub>i</sub><sup>†</sup></em> − α* <em>a<sub>i</sub></em>); it translates the coherent amplitude by α in phase space.

A single-mode squeezer is S(ξ) = exp((ξ*<em>a</em>² − ξ <em>a<sup>†</sup></em>²)/2) with ξ = re<sup>iθ</sup>. It rescales the quadratures x<sub>θ/2</sub> → e<sup>−r</sup>x<sub>θ/2</sub> and x<sub>θ/2+π/2</sub> → e<sup>r</sup>x<sub>θ/2+π/2</sub>, conserving the uncertainty product. The two-mode squeezer S<sub>2</sub>(ξ) = exp(ξ*<em>a<sub>1</sub>a<sub>2</sub></em> − ξ <em>a<sub>1</sub><sup>†</sup>a<sub>2</sub><sup>†</sup></em>) entangles two modes by squeezing the joint quadrature.

The right structural object is the real symplectic group Sp(2N, R). A Gaussian unitary acts on the 2N-dimensional vector of quadratures (x<sub>1</sub>, p<sub>1</sub>, ..., x<sub>N</sub>, p<sub>N</sub>) as a symplectic matrix S satisfying S<sup>T</sup>ΩS = Ω. The Bloch–Messiah decomposition factors any such S as S = O<sub>1</sub> · diag(e<sup>r<sub>i</sub></sup>, e<sup>−r<sub>i</sub></sup>) · O<sub>2</sub>, where O<sub>1</sub> and O<sub>2</sub> are passive (energy-preserving) symplectic transformations and the diagonal block is a tensor product of single-mode squeezers.

## Decomposition: Reck and Clements

The passive blocks O<sub>1</sub> and O<sub>2</sub> are exactly N-mode linear-optical unitaries acting only on the annihilation operators. Reck et al. (1994) showed that any such N×N unitary can be implemented as a triangular mesh of N(N−1)/2 MZIs<sup className="fn-ref"><a href="#fn-1">[1]</a></sup>. Clements et al. (2016) gave a rectangular alternative with the same MZI count but half the optical depth and more uniform loss<sup className="fn-ref"><a href="#fn-2">[2]</a></sup>.

The full recipe for any Gaussian unitary on N modes is therefore: a Clements mesh, then a layer of single-mode squeezers, then a second Clements mesh, then a layer of displacements. The mesh angles and squeezing parameters are determined by Bloch–Messiah on the target symplectic matrix.

## Hardware today

Silicon nitride and silicon-on-insulator MZI meshes are the current workhorses for the passive blocks, with thermo-optic or electro-optic phase shifters tuning the internal angles. Typical per-MZI insertion loss on SiN at telecom wavelengths sits in the 0.1–0.5 dB range in demonstrated devices, with thin-film lithium niobate offering faster modulation at comparable loss<sup className="fn-ref"><a href="#fn-3">[3]</a></sup>. Calibration of an N-mode mesh requires per-MZI characterization because fabrication variation drifts the splitter ratios away from 50:50.

Squeezed light is generated by parametric processes: degenerate optical parametric amplification for single-mode squeezing, non-degenerate four-wave mixing in microring resonators for two-mode squeezing. Bulk OPO setups have demonstrated roughly 15 dB of measured squeezing under ideal conditions; integrated platforms currently deliver about 3–6 dB on chip, limited by propagation loss and pump-cleanup constraints. Xanadu's Borealis demonstrated a programmable Gaussian boson sampler with time-multiplexed squeezed states and 216 modes<sup className="fn-ref"><a href="#fn-4">[4]</a></sup>, a proof-of-concept rather than a fault-tolerant device.

Displacements are realized by interfering the signal mode with a strong coherent local oscillator on a highly asymmetric beam splitter. This is routine; the engineering effort is concentrated in low-loss squeezing and phase-stable mesh control.

## The Gaussian classical-simulability boundary

A circuit built only from Gaussian unitaries, acting on Gaussian input states, and read out with Gaussian (homodyne or heterodyne) detection, can be simulated in polynomial time on a classical computer. Why? Because the entire state is captured by a 2N-dimensional mean vector and a 2N×2N covariance matrix, and both evolve through the circuit by ordinary symplectic matrix arithmetic. That's it. No exponential blowup; just linear algebra in a laptop-sized space.

Bartlett, Sanders, Braunstein, and de Guise (2002) proved the simulability result for Gaussian measurements<sup className="fn-ref"><a href="#fn-5">[5]</a></sup>. Mari and Eisert (2012) sharpened the boundary by showing that negativity of the Wigner function is a necessary resource for any quantum speedup in continuous-variable systems<sup className="fn-ref"><a href="#fn-6">[6]</a></sup>. Gaussian states have non-negative Wigner functions, so they cannot supply that resource.

A Gaussian-only photonic chip, then, is a very sophisticated classical analog computer for symplectic linear algebra: impressive, but not a quantum computer in any meaningful sense. To escape simulability you have to add a non-Gaussian ingredient: a non-Gaussian input state (Fock, cat, GKP), a non-Gaussian gate (cubic phase), or a non-Gaussian measurement (photon-number-resolving detection, as in Gaussian boson sampling). One way or another, you have to smuggle some negativity in. Chapter 04 is about which smugglers we actually have and how much they charge.
